Kinda Easy Hack

3rik 3l rojo
2026-02-19
Day-Project, Write-up

Some of my friends are really good at this demonic game. Since I’m not a very skilled gamer, it’s impossible for me to win fairly. But maybe I can win unfairly. Let’s try sending our own better score.

How is the score sent?

Time to fire up Burp Suite and start inspecting the HTTPS requests. I had to play at least once, but I ended up playing eight times to gather more data. In the image below, you can see one of the POST requests. This request is triggered when you complete the level of the day. It’s an API call named submit-score, so it’s a no-brainer that this will be a crucial part of the hacking process.

There’s a lot of information in the request, but the most important part is the data at the end. We have pretty straightforward names: score, infuriatingMode, isFirstAttempt; and one strange field: q.

Let’s try sending this request to the repeater and changing the score to 1. We do it, send it, and—done—the response is “Score submitted!”. I check the scoreboard, and of course, it’s not that easy. The server performs a check and discards our score without telling us. This might slow us down because we now have to verify manually, but it also tells us that q is probably something we should focus on.

What is q?

q is likely some kind of hash based on the other fields. But how can we confirm this? Let’s achieve the same score multiple times. The JSON below represents the data sent each time I finished the game. Notice that the last two times, the score and q are identical:
